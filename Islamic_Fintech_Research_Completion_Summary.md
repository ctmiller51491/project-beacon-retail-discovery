## Key Research Findings

### Finding 1: Market Maturation
Islamic fintech has evolved from experimental to mainstream, with $138B (2022) projected to reach $306-341B by 2027-2029.

### Finding 2: SSB Model Essential
All major platforms employ Sharia Supervisory Boards as operational governance bodies, not merely advisory committees.

### Finding 3: Contract Precision Required
Success depends on exact alignment between contract design and digital implementation; inconsistencies create compliance gaps.

### Finding 4: Two-Tier Screening Standard
Investment platforms universally employ business activity + financial ratio screening methodology.

### Finding 5: AAOIFI Alignment
AAOIFI standards serve as de facto compliance framework, with adoption increasing across platform types.

### Finding 6: Regional Variations
Regulatory approaches differ significantly by jurisdiction, requiring platform-specific compliance strategies.

### Finding 7: Neobank Emergence
Purpose-built Islamic digital banks (MyMy, ATLAS, AEON) represent new paradigm for Sharia-compliant fintech.

### Finding 8: Sukuk Tokenization
Blockchain-based Sukuk fractionalizes ownership and improves liquidity, transforming capital markets structure.

### Finding 9: Zakat Digitalization
Digital platforms improve Zakat collection efficiency and transparency through automation and real-time tracking.

### Finding 10: Embedded Finance Extension
Islamic finance integrating into lifestyle platforms (e-commerce, travel, food) extends Sharia-compliant options.

### Finding 11: AI Complements Human Judgment
Automation improves efficiency but cannot replace Sharia scholar expertise for complex products and novel structures.

### Finding 12: Regulatory Sandboxes Enable Innovation
Leading regulators use sandboxes to support innovation while maintaining Sharia compliance standards.
